The mechanism of the rotating system is designed to guide flies into a central death chamber using coordinated moving components. The reaper wall and bottom peg plate work together to converge flies toward the center of the trap, where they are then dropped into the death chamber through a rotating floor. The system consists of several key components, including a D-profile rotary shaft made of carbon steel, a motor operating at 3V and 5 RPM, and multiple 3D-printed PLA parts such as the death chamber, reaper wall with six pegs, motor attachment lid, peg plate with 28 pegs, motor shaft connector, and rotating floor. The shaft is cut to a length of six inches with a diameter of 0.25 inches, while most structural components are fabricated through 3D printing.

The assembly functions by having the peg plate secured into a housing connected to the death chamber, where its pegs interlock with those on the reaper wall to guide flies inward. The motor drives the D-profile shaft, which rotates the reaper wall and the attached rotating floor. The motor is mounted onto a lid using bolts, and a shaft connector links the motor shaft to the D-profile shaft. As the rotating floor spins, it periodically creates an opening that allows flies to fall into the death chamber, while surrounding walls prevent them from escaping elsewhere. Some components, such as the motor-shaft connector and shaft collar, were not included in the functional prototype since they were not required at the current testing stage.

Several tests were conducted to evaluate the design. In the peg movement test using crumbled paper balls as fly substitutes, the system failed to guide the balls effectively, instead crushing them regardless of their starting position. As a result, the next design iteration will include increasing the curvature of the peg path to improve guidance. In the chamber drop test, the system performed successfully, with paper balls consistently falling into the death chamber in all five trials. Future improvements include increasing the hole diameter to allow multiple flies to pass through simultaneously and adding a roof to prevent escape. During the manual spin test, the system rotated but experienced resistance when the reaper wall passed through the pegs, causing instability and occasional movement of the entire device. To address this, additional weight may be added to the base for stability, and peg tolerances may be adjusted to reduce friction.

The success criteria for the project focus on reliability, smooth operation, structural stability, and efficiency. Reliable transfer is measured by the percentage of successful movements into the chamber, while smooth operation is assessed through observation of friction and uninterrupted rotation. Structural stability is evaluated based on whether the system remains intact and steady during use, and efficiency is determined by the time taken for a fly (or paper ball) to reach the chamber. Smooth mechanical motion is especially important for demonstration purposes, as it reflects the system’s durability and effectiveness. Overall, the prototype demonstrates a functional concept with clear areas identified for improvement in future iterations.
